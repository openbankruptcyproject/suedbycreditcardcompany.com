Sued by a Credit Card Company?

Don't Panic. Here's Exactly What to Do.

You Just Got Served -- Now What?

Take a breath. Being sued by a credit card company is stressful but it is not an emergency that requires immediate action beyond one thing: note the deadline to respond. You typically have 20-30 days (varies by state) to file an answer with the court. Missing this deadline results in a default judgment.

Step 1: Read the Complaint Carefully

The complaint will state: who is suing you (original creditor or debt buyer), the amount claimed, the account number, and the legal basis. Check every detail. Is the amount correct? Is this actually your account? Has the statute of limitations expired? Check your state's statute of limitations.

Step 2: File an Answer

Even if you owe the money, file an answer. Respond to each paragraph: admit, deny, or state you lack sufficient information. Raise affirmative defenses: statute of limitations, lack of standing (if sued by debt buyer), improper service, incorrect amount.

Many courts have fill-in-the-blank answer forms. Check your local court website.

Step 3: Request Validation

If a debt buyer is suing you (not the original creditor), demand they prove they own the debt. They must produce the original credit agreement, a complete chain of ownership, and an accounting of every charge and payment. Many debt buyers cannot produce these documents. Cease and desist guide.

Common Defenses

Statute of limitations: If the debt is time-barred, the lawsuit fails.

Lack of standing: Debt buyers must prove they own the specific account.

Wrong amount: Challenge interest, fees, and charges that were not in the original agreement.

Identity: Sometimes the wrong person is sued.

Already paid or settled: Bring proof of any payments or settlement agreements.

The Bankruptcy Option

Filing bankruptcy immediately stops the lawsuit through the automatic stay. If you have multiple debts and this lawsuit is just one problem among many, bankruptcy may be the most efficient solution. Chapter 7 eliminates the credit card debt entirely. The automatic stay explained.

What Happens If You Lose

If a judgment is entered against you, the creditor can garnish wages (up to 25% of disposable income), levy bank accounts, and place liens on property. Bankruptcy can still eliminate judgment debt and stop garnishment even after a judgment is entered. Are you judgment proof?

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to respond to a credit card lawsuit?

Typically 20-30 days from the date you are served, depending on your state. Check the summons for the exact deadline.

Can I negotiate a settlement after being sued?

Yes. Many creditors will settle for 40-60% of the balance even after filing suit. Get any agreement in writing before paying.

What if I cannot afford a lawyer?

Many legal aid organizations help with debt defense. You can also represent yourself (pro se). The court clerk can provide answer forms.

Will bankruptcy stop the lawsuit?

Yes, immediately. The automatic stay halts all litigation the moment you file.
